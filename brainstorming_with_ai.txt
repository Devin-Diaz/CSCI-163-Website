Your project in one sentence

A mini website for a lay audience explaining how AI coding assistants (chatbots/Copilot/Cursor) are reshaping CS learning, grading, and hiring—who benefits, who’s harmed, and what we should do about it.

1) Professor requirements → exact “website deliverables” checklist
Requirement A: Educate a lay audience

✅ Your site must clearly explain:

What AI coding assistants are (LLMs, IDE copilots, “vibe coding”)

What they’re used for in CS learning (generate code, explain code, debug, autocomplete)

Where they show up (homework, labs, projects, interviews)

Website implementation boxes

 “What these tools are” page/section (plain English + examples)

 “Where students use them” page/section (school + interviews)

 Quick glossary (LLM, prompt, autocomplete, hallucination, etc.)

Requirement B: Provoke critical thinking about design/use + impacts

✅ Your site must actively push visitors to question fairness + consequences:

“Is this learning or outsourcing thinking?”

“Who can afford the best tools?”

“Do bans and surveillance harm some groups more than others?”

“Are current assessments measuring real ability?”

Website implementation boxes

 “Who gains / who loses?” section (access, inequality, skill gaps)

 “Assessment & cheating vs learning” section

 “Surveillance vs education” section (detectors, bans, monitoring)

Requirement C: Inspire reflection or action

✅ Your site must do more than inform—it must lead to reflection or next steps.

Website implementation boxes

 “What should change?” section (your proposed approach)

 “Take action” section with concrete suggestions for:

students (how to use AI responsibly to learn)

instructors (assessment redesign, AI policy)

schools (AI literacy support, equity considerations)

 A short “reflection prompt” block (questions people answer mentally before survey)

Requirement D: Use at least 2 course readings + 4 outside scholarly sources

✅ You must incorporate them either:

directly on the site (citations + quotes/paraphrases), OR

via a 1–3 page single-spaced reflection explaining how sources informed your artifact.

Strong recommendation (clean + easy):

Put light citations on the site + a separate reflection page (or PDF) with fuller explanation.

Website implementation boxes

 Works Cited page (all sources listed)

 In-text citations or footnotes on key claims

 Reflection component (1–3 pages single-spaced) explaining:

which 2 course readings you used

which 4 scholarly sources you used

where each influenced the website

(I can help you build a citation plan once you tell me the 2 course readings you want to use.)

Requirement E: Anonymous survey + at least 10 responses by March 21 + analyze

✅ You must create an anonymous survey to measure:

what people learned

what can be improved
…and you must collect/analyze ≥ 10 responses.

Website implementation boxes

 Survey link embedded prominently (Google Forms is easiest)

 “Survey consent blurb” (anonymous, voluntary, purpose)

 A “Results” section/page where you summarize findings (after you collect them)

 Basic analysis: charts + 5–10 bullet takeaways + “what I would improve”

2) Suggested website structure (minimum viable but strong)

You can keep it simple (single-page scroll) or multi-page. Multi-page is easier to keep organized:

Page 1 — Home / Hook

Short scenario + 2–3 key questions

CTA buttons: “Learn the Issue” / “Take the Survey”

Page 2 — The Tools (Education requirement)

LLM chatbots vs IDE copilots vs vibe coding

1–2 safe examples: “Here’s what it can do”

Page 3 — Impacts (Critical thinking requirement)

Split into clear cards:

Learning outcomes (understanding vs completion)

Fairness/access (paid tools, prior experience)

Academic integrity (what is “cheating” now?)

Technical interviews (new forms of dishonesty + new forms of gatekeeping)

Page 4 — Surveillance vs Education (Power + ethics)

Detectors, bans, monitoring

Tradeoffs and who gets harmed

Page 5 — Solutions

Two parts:

“What people propose already” (summarize 2–3)

“My proposal” (yours) with pros/cons

Page 6 — Survey

Embed or link the form + short consent blurb

Page 7 — Works Cited (+ optional Reflection)

Works Cited required

Reflection component optional but recommended

3) Data you can gather and show (to make the website feel “real”)

You don’t need a huge dataset. You need credible + visual evidence.

A) Your own anonymous survey data (required anyway)

Collect:

prior familiarity with AI tools

whether they think AI helps learning or hurts it

perceptions of fairness

whether your website changed their view

How to represent on site (after March 21)

bar charts of key questions

2–3 anonymized short responses (no identifying info)

a “What I’d improve” section based on feedback

B) Mini “user study” style interaction (optional but cool)

On the site, include one interactive element:

A short quiz: “Is this learning or outsourcing?”

A slider: “Where do you think the line is between help and cheating?”

A choose-your-path scenario: “You’re a student/instructor/employer—what policy do you choose?”

This helps you satisfy “provoke critical thinking” in a measurable way.

C) Scholarly stats (outside sources)

Pull a few simple, high-impact figures from papers/reports like:

% of students using AI tools for programming tasks

measured effects on learning outcomes (if studied)

error rates / hallucination issues in coding assistants

impacts of surveillance tools (false positives, trust issues)

Then show:

2–4 small charts or “stat cards” with citations

D) “Policy examples” (lightweight evidence)

Show 2–3 examples of how institutions respond:

AI allowed with disclosure

banned

redesigned assessments

Even a simple comparison table works (and you cite sources).
